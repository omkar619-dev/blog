---
title: This Time for Africa
description: Why the 2010 World Cup — the first ever held on African soil — still feels like the most human, most emotional, closest-to-world-peace tournament football has ever produced.
---

Close your eyes and you can still hear it. That low, endless, oceanic *drone* — a hundred thousand vuvuzelas blurring into one sound, like the whole continent had been given a single voice and decided to just *hum*. Over the top of it, Shakira and a South African band singing a melody that was older than any of us, and a Somali refugee's son singing about a flag waving over the free. The summer of 2010 had a soundtrack, and fourteen years later I only have to hear three seconds of it to be right back there.

You said it felt like the closest we've ever come to world peace. I want to argue that you're not exaggerating — and explain why a tournament with middling football and an unloved ball is, to this day, the most *important* World Cup ever played.

## Eighty years, and never once here

Start with the fact that's so big it's easy to walk past: **the World Cup had existed since 1930, and in eighty years it had never once been held in Africa.**

Think about what that means. For eight decades, Africa was the place football was *exported to* and the place talent was *extracted from* — the continent that filled Europe's best teams and was, somehow, never trusted to host the party itself. Every World Cup had been in Europe or the Americas. Africa watched from the outside of its own family.

2010 ended that. For the first time, the planet's biggest event came *home* to the continent that loves the game as fiercely as anywhere on earth. The slogan said it plainly — *Ke Nako*, Sesotho for "it's time." It was a correction of an eighty-year absence, and everyone watching knew it. That alone would have made it historic. What happened around it made it sacred.

## A nation that already knew how to heal

And of all the places on the continent, it landed in the one with the heaviest, most hopeful story to tell.

South Africa in 2010 was *sixteen years* out of apartheid. Sixteen. A country that, within living memory, had been engineered to keep its own people apart was now inviting the entire world in and saying: *come, all of you, together, here.* The Rainbow Nation hosting the planet was not a marketing line. It was a statement of arrival, and a dare to its own past.

Hanging over all of it was one man. Nelson Mandela had helped win the bid; this was meant to be his crowning gift to his country. Then, the night before the opening ceremony, his great-granddaughter Zenani was killed in a car crash on her way home from a pre-tournament concert. He stayed away from the opening in grief. And so when he finally appeared — at the final, bundled against the Johannesburg winter cold, riding out onto the Soccer City pitch on a cart, frail and luminous, waving to a roaring world — it broke your heart and lifted it at the same time. It was the last great public moment of his life. *Madiba*, who had used sport once before to stitch a fractured nation together, doing it one final time, for a whole continent.

There's a word for the thing the country kept talking about that summer: *Ubuntu*. "I am because we are." You felt it in the stadiums. The whole tournament felt like Ubuntu set to music.

## The soundtrack of a continent

Because the music — God, the music.

"Waka Waka (This Time for Africa)" wasn't just a pop song bolted onto a sponsorship deal. Its hook was lifted from "Zangaléwa," a song by Cameroonian soldiers from the 1980s — meaning the anthem the world danced to was *African music going global*, not a foreign tune dressed up in kente cloth. And "Wavin' Flag," sung by K'naan, a kid who'd fled the war in Somalia — a refugee's son writing the song a billion people sang about freedom and flags. These were not background tracks. They were the emotional grammar of the whole event.

And the vuvuzela. People complained — fair enough, it could drive you mad through a TV speaker. But that sound was the tournament's heartbeat, and it was *unmistakably, defiantly African*. No previous World Cup had a sound. This one did, and it refused to apologise for it. You hear that drone now and your body knows exactly where and when it is.

## Tshabalala, and the goal that opened the sky

The first goal of the tournament told you everything about why it mattered.

Opening match, South Africa against Mexico, Soccer City packed and shaking. Second half, the ball breaks to Siphiwe Tshabalala on the left, and he *leathers* it into the top corner — a strike so clean it seemed to tear the roof off the sky. Bafana Bafana, the host nation, ahead in the first game of Africa's first World Cup. The players peeled off into a choreographed dance by the corner flag and a continent lost its mind.

South Africa would go on to become the first host nation ever knocked out in the group stage. Didn't matter. That goal is immortal. For one afternoon, the hosts gave their people the purest joy a home crowd can feel, and no group-stage table can take it back.

## Ghana: a whole continent's heart, in one penalty

But the emotional core of 2010 — the moment that still aches — belongs to Ghana.

One by one the African teams went out. Nigeria, Cameroon, Ivory Coast (Drogba playing with his arm in a cast), Algeria, the hosts. By the quarterfinals only Ghana was left — and so Ghana stopped being Ghana's team and became *Africa's* team. A continent of a billion people poured every ounce of hope into the Black Stars. Win, and they'd be the first African side ever to reach a World Cup semifinal, on African soil, in Africa's World Cup. You couldn't have scripted higher stakes.

Quarterfinal, Soccer City, against Uruguay. 1–1 deep into extra time. The very last seconds. Ghana send a header goalbound — and Luis Suárez, standing on the line, *stops it with his hands*. Deliberate. Red card, penalty, and a continent on its feet because this was it, this was the moment.

Asamoah Gyan steps up. And hits the crossbar.

The width of a crossbar. That's how close Africa came. Suárez, sprinting off down the tunnel, turned and *celebrated* the miss — and became, depending on which half of the planet you stood on, the greatest villain or the most ruthless winner of the tournament. Ghana lost the shootout. And the dream of a continent died, in its own house, by inches.

I've never quite gotten over that one. But notice even the heartbreak: Africa wasn't a footnote in someone else's tournament. Africa was the *protagonist*. The whole world held its breath for Ghana. That had never happened before either.

## The boys who walked out as legends

And the football did give us a changing of the guard.

A baby-faced Germany — Müller, Özil, Khedira, Schweinsteiger — played with a fearless freedom and dismantled England 4–1 (off a Lampard shot that *clearly* crossed the line and wasn't given, the injustice that finally shamed football into goal-line technology) and then took Argentina apart 4–0. Thomas Müller walked in unknown and walked out with the Golden Boot. Diego Forlán dragged Uruguay to their best finish since 1970 and took the Golden Ball. Sneijder was everywhere.

Meanwhile the old kings crumbled: defending champions Italy, gone in the group stage; France imploding in open mutiny, players refusing to train. The torch was visibly changing hands.

And at the end of it, the coronation: **Spain's first-ever World Cup.** Iniesta's right boot, the 116th minute, the whole of *tiki-taka* — Xavi, Iniesta, Villa, Casillas — reaching its summit. A new dynasty crowned in the old continent's first African summer.

## The honest part: it wasn't the *best* football

I have to be straight, the way I was about joga bonito, or this is just nostalgia wearing a nice shirt.

2010 was, by the numbers, a *poor* football tournament. Low-scoring. The Jabulani ball flew like a balloon and goalkeepers hated it. The final itself was wretched — a cynical, card-strewn brawl where a Dutch midfielder planted his studs in Xabi Alonso's chest and somehow stayed on the pitch, and Spain ground out 1–0s with more control than thrill. And yes, the vuvuzela genuinely drove a lot of people up the wall.

There's a harder truth too, underneath the glow: the eye-watering cost, the stadiums that became white elephants the moment the world left, the gap between one shimmering month and the poverty that still sat outside the gates. A World Cup is not a country, and the party didn't fix what the cameras flew over.

So if the football was middling and the legacy was complicated — why does it still feel *perfect*?

## Because it was never about the football

Because for one month, the whole world *chose to gather* — in peace, on purpose — in a place that, more than anywhere, symbolised humanity clawing its way past its own worst instincts. And it *worked*.

Every quiet, condescending doubt — the whispers that Africa "couldn't" pull it off, the concern-trolling about whether it was "ready" — was answered by a tournament that was warm, safe, joyous, and beautiful in every way that doesn't show up on a scoreboard. The doubters were simply, completely wrong, and the continent got to prove it on the biggest stage that exists.

That's the feeling you're describing. The closest-to-world-peace thing. For a few weeks the planet felt like one room, everyone humming on the same impossible note, and the room happened to be in Africa, and an old man who'd spent twenty-seven years in a cell got to wave at it before he went. We have not felt that since. I'm not sure we will again.

It wasn't the best World Cup. It was the most *human* one. And it was exactly, perfectly, where it needed to be.

*Ke nako.* It was time. And for one summer, it really did feel like it was time for all of us.

— *Still humming, all these years later.*
