---
title: Who Killed Joga Bonito?
description: A love letter and an autopsy — how football traded the unshackled magician for the efficient machine, and whether the beautiful game lost its soul or just changed shape.
---

There's a version of football I fell in love with that I'm not sure exists anymore.

It had a sound to it — the commentary cracking an octave higher because Ronaldinho had just done *something*, some flick or elastico or no-look pass that made no tactical sense and all the sense in the world. It had a colour: Brazil yellow, Barça's stripes, the Nike ads where grown men juggled like kids in a parking lot. *Joga Bonito.* Play beautifully. Not "play efficiently." Not "control the half-spaces." Play *beautifully*.

Those were the days of Kaká gliding through midfield like the grass was tilted in his favour. Of R9 — the real Ronaldo, the *Fenômeno* — doing things at full sprint that defenders are still apologising for. Of Dinho's grin, Robinho's stepovers, Roberto Carlos bending physics from forty yards, Adriano hitting shots that should've come with a warning label. Football then felt like it was *played by people*. Now it often feels like it's *executed by systems*.

So let me ask the question that's been rattling around my head: **who killed joga bonito?** Because something did. And I don't think it was one killer. I think it was a conspiracy.

## Suspect #1: The tyranny of the system

The single biggest culprit is the thing everyone now praises — *the system*.

Modern elite football is a tactical machine. Positional play, gegenpressing, automatisms, "rest defence," inverted full-backs, the half-space, the third man. Every blade of grass is accounted for. Every player has a job, a zone, a trigger. It is genuinely brilliant — Pep Guardiola's teams move like a single organism, and that's its own kind of art.

But here's the cost: **a system has no room for improvisation, because improvisation is just another word for unpredictability, and unpredictability is what systems are built to eliminate.** The old No. 10 — Riquelme, Totti, the playmaker who slowed the game down and did whatever he felt like — has no home in a structure that needs every player pressing in unison two seconds after losing the ball. You can't drift and dream when you have a defensive shape to hold.

The magicians weren't coached. They were *tolerated*. Today they'd be told to track back.

## Suspect #2: The data

Then came the spreadsheets.

xG. Expected threat. Pass completion under pressure. Football discovered Moneyball, and Moneyball discovered that flair is *inefficient*. A rainbow flick has a lower expected value than a simple square pass. The 35-yard screamer is, statistically, a bad shot — you should've recycled possession. The no-look pass is a turnover risk.

So we optimised. And optimisation, by its nature, sands the edges off. It rewards the boring-but-correct over the spectacular-but-low-percentage. Adriano's worldies, R9's solo runs, Roberto Carlos's free kick against France — half of those were "bad decisions" by the model. They were also the entire point.

We didn't just measure the game. We started letting the measurement coach it.

## Suspect #3: The athletes

The players got faster, fitter, and stronger — and the ball spent less and less time at anyone's feet.

In Ronaldinho's prime, a playmaker might get three, four seconds on the ball to look up and invent. Now? You get one touch and a swarm. The pressing intensity of the modern game means there is *physically no time* for a stepover when three pressers collapse on you in a heartbeat. Skill has been replaced — or rather, redefined — as the ability to release the ball in 0.4 seconds before contact.

The classic *enganche*, the slow genius who needed space and time, is extinct because the modern game grants neither. We bred athletes and starved artists.

## Suspect #4: The money

And underneath it all — fear.

There's more money in football now than ever, and money makes people cautious. A relegation costs hundreds of millions. A manager gets sacked in October. When the stakes are that high, you don't *adventure* — you *manage risk*. You park the bus, you keep it tight, you take the draw. Pragmatism isn't a tactic; it's a survival instinct.

Joga bonito was, in a way, a luxury of a slightly more innocent era — when losing beautifully was still better than winning ugly. Try telling a modern boardroom that.

## Suspect #5: The academy machine

The last killer is the saddest, because it strikes at the root.

Brazilian flair didn't come from academies. It came from *futsal* and the *street* — small spaces, bad surfaces, no coaches, infinite touches, and the only currency that mattered being whether you could beat your man and humiliate him doing it. That's where the elastico was born. That's where joy was the syllabus.

Now talent gets identified at eight and fed into an academy conveyor belt that coaches for *positional discipline and decision-making efficiency* from the start. We catch the wild thing early and we domesticate it. We produce technically immaculate, tactically obedient players — and far fewer beautiful lunatics.

You can't manufacture a Ronaldinho in a lab. You have to leave a kid alone with a ball on a broken pitch and let him fall in love.

## The honest part: am I just old?

Here's where I have to be fair to myself, because nostalgia is a liar.

We remember the highlight reels, not the 0-0s. The 2000s had plenty of cynical, ugly, defensive football too — catenaccio didn't die, route-one existed, and for every Ronaldinho night there were ten forgettable midtable slogs we've conveniently deleted from memory. The brain keeps the magic and bins the boredom, then tells you the past was all magic.

And the present isn't a wasteland. The *technical* level today is higher than it has ever been — these players are absurd. Messi spanned both eras and arguably perfected the bridge. Vinícius dances. Lamine Yamal, at seventeen, plays like he never got the memo that the street was dead. City's passing patterns, at their best, are genuinely beautiful — just a *collective* beauty instead of an individual one.

So maybe joga bonito wasn't murdered. Maybe it just changed shape — from the chaos of one man beating four, to the choreography of eleven men moving as one.

## What we actually traded

But I'll plant my flag here anyway, because it's *my* blog and these are *my* random thoughts: even granting all of that, **something real was lost, and it's worth naming.**

We traded the beauty of *chaos* for the beauty of *control*. We traded the unshackled individual for the efficient collective. We traded the player who could win a match alone, on a whim, for a system that wins matches reliably and forgettably. Both are beautiful. Only one made me leap off the sofa.

Joga bonito wasn't killed in a single blow. It was *optimised away* — death by a thousand marginal gains. Each one defensible. Each one smart. And the sum of all of them is a game that is better-run, better-drilled, more sophisticated, and just a little less *alive*.

The soul of football was never the trophies. It was the moment your jaw dropped and you didn't know why a human body could do that. It was Dinho's grin. It was R9 at full flight. It was a yellow shirt deciding, mid-match, to do something impossible just because it was fun.

I still watch. I still love it. But I watch a little more with my head now and a little less with my heart — and I think the game made that trade *for* me, not *with* me.

Still. Every so often a kid like Yamal jinks past two men and grins, and for ninety seconds the street comes back to life. So maybe it's not dead. Maybe it's just sleeping, waiting for the next broken pitch and the next kid who never learned he's supposed to play efficiently.

Long live joga bonito. Whoever's keeping it alive — thank you.
